Here we explain how stars are born in nebulae

Protostar from nebula

Description

Stars are born in dense regions within nebulae, which are vast clouds of gas and dust in interstellar space, often called molecular clouds or stellar nurseries.

​The process of star formation can be summarized in the following stages:

1. Gravitational Collapse

Star formation begins when denser regions within a cold molecular cloud experience a disturbance (such as a shockwave from a nearby supernova or stellar winds) that causes them to become unstable. ​Gravity then causes these dense clumps of gas and dust to contract and collapse inward. As the clump's mass grows, its gravitational force strengthens, pulling in more surrounding matter.

2. Protostar Formation

As the cloud collapses, the material is squeezed into a smaller volume. The friction from the collapsing matter converts gravitational potential energy into heat, causing the central region to get progressively hotter and denser. ​This hot, dense core is known as a protostar. It is a baby star that is not yet hot enough to begin nuclear fusion. ​The protostar is surrounded by a rotating disk of gas and dust, which may eventually form planets (a protoplanetary disk), and it often shoots out powerful jets of material from its poles.

3. Ignition of Nuclear Fusion

The gravitational collapse continues, increasing the temperature and pressure in the protostar's core. This stage can take millions of years. ​When the core temperature reaches about 10 million Kelvin (approximately 18 million degrees Fahrenheit), the immense pressure forces hydrogen nuclei to fuse into helium in a process called nuclear fusion. ​The energy released by nuclear fusion creates a powerful outward pressure that finally balances the inward pull of gravity, halting the collapse. The object is now a true main sequence star, where it will spend the majority of its life. ​The entire process begins with a stellar nebula, which acts as the raw material for a new star.

Here are the of the most famous star-forming regions:

  • Orion Nebula (M42): The closest major star formation region to Earth (about 1,400 light-years away) and a frequent target for telescopes. It contains the Trapezium Cluster of hot, young stars and is rich in proplyds (protoplanetary discs), which are planetary systems in the making.

  • Carina Nebula (NGC 3372): One of the largest and brightest nebulae in our galaxy, containing some of the most massive and luminous stars known, including the highly unstable hypergiant star, Eta Carinae. It's much larger than the Orion Nebula.

  • Eagle Nebula (M16): Famous for the iconic image known as the Pillars of Creation, tall columns of gas and dust that are actively incubating new stars. This region is about 7,000 light-years away in the constellation Serpens.

  • Tarantula Nebula (30 Doradus): The most massive and active star-forming region in the entire Local Group of galaxies. It is located in the Large Magellanic Cloud, a small companion galaxy to the Milky Way, and contains thousands of massive stars.

  • Rho Ophiuchi Cloud Complex: One of the nearest star nurseries, located about 400–500 light-years away in the constellation Ophiuchus. Its proximity makes it an excellent target for studying the early stages of star formation.

Key Concepts

  • Nebula: The initial cloud of gas and dust.
  • Gravity: The force that drives the initial collapse.
  • Nuclear Fusion: The process that powers the star and marks the birth of a main sequence star.

The universe is dotted with giant nurseries of gas and dust where new stars are constantly being born. These dense molecular clouds are some of the most famous and spectacular objects in the night sky.
